GoHeartBeat — Founding Engineer · Sept 2024–Present

GoHeartBeat is a B2B SaaS analytics platform for startups — the idea being that founders shouldn't have to route every data question through analysts and wait days for a stale number. The founders were the ex-Toppr CEO and CTO. I knew how they built. I joined at roughly half my prior salary in exchange for equity and the chance to start from nothing.

There was no codebase, no infrastructure, no company. I designed the full stack — Golang and Python services on AWS EKS, Postgres, ClickHouse as the analytical layer for clients without a warehouse, CubeJS as a semantic layer with pre-aggregation for sub-250ms query performance — and shipped a working version to the first pilot client in three months. Production-ready in six.

The most consequential decision was one nobody forced. Enterprise and finance clients have strict data residency requirements: their data cannot route through a third party's infrastructure. Standard SaaS architecture fails that test entirely. Before any client raised the constraint, I designed a compliance-first multi-tenant model where ClickHouse and CubeJS deploy inside the client's own AWS account, and our application layer calls their CubeJS for pre-aggregated results only — raw data never leaves their environment. When the first enterprise client arrived with that exact requirement, the architecture was already in place. That deal closed at ₹15L/month. A conventional setup would have lost it.

The same constraint made every off-the-shelf data pipeline tool unworkable — Fivetran and Airbyte both route data through their own infrastructure, which violated the residency model. Neither had connectors for the Indian SaaS APIs we needed. I designed a three-part replacement: a central orchestrator that manages schedules, credentials, and job dispatch without ever touching client data; a connector library where integrations are reusable Python classes rather than DAGs; and a lightweight agent that runs inside the client's AWS account, pulls jobs outbound, executes connectors locally, and writes to their ClickHouse. Purely outbound — no inbound connections into the client VPC.

Later I built Echo, a conversational analytics layer that translates natural-language questions into CubeJS queries and streams results back as prose. The non-trivial part was token cost: a CubeJS schema for a single client runs to 200K+ characters. Injecting it on every LLM call was prohibitive. Schema-aware session management — injecting once at session start and reusing across turns — cut token usage by 78%. The platform grew to six clients and contributed to a $1M fundraise. I'm leaving because equity terms agreed at joining didn't materialise, not because the product is in trouble.

Whitehat Jr. — Principal Software Engineer · Aug 2022–Aug 2024

Whitehat Jr. ran live 1:1 coding classes for kids, had been acquired by Byju's, and by the time I arrived had been largely hollowed out. The original engineering team had scattered post-acquisition. What remained was about fifteen engineers, mostly junior, no senior technical direction, and a product that was generating public incidents regularly. The Toppr CTO had been handed operational responsibility for the product and pulled me in — not through a job post, but because of trust built at Toppr.

I made a deliberate choice not to walk in as the leader. I told the team I wanted to work alongside them as a developer first — understand the codebase, experience the incidents, see the reality before taking charge. The last senior engineer with institutional knowledge was days from leaving his notice period. I extracted everything I could from him, established back-channels with ex-Whitehat engineers now at Byju's central, and worked through the immediate fires: OTP API abuse hammering Twilio costs, a communications engine sending ten-plus reminders per class with many never delivered, class join failures caused by slot logic producing invalid states. For join failures, the triage model was: issue a fallback video link immediately to keep the class running, then diagnose the root cause in parallel.

Once the acute fires were contained, one incident pattern remained. The same failure, at the same times, every day — peak class hours. The assumption in the team was that there were more bugs to find. The reality: infrastructure was scaling too late. Auto-scaling was reactive, triggered when thresholds were breached, by which point load had already hit. New instances took two to three minutes to warm up. During those minutes: classes failing, teachers and students dropping. The complexity wasn't obvious — classes from the current hour extend into the next, students join five minutes early, and India and Brazil have overlapping peak profiles that both had to be accounted for. I analysed a month of historical class data, derived the actual overlap and early-join factors, and rebuilt scaling to look ahead at scheduled load rather than react to current load. Instances were warm before peak arrived. That one change eliminated 90% of all platform incidents.

In parallel I ran cost reduction across three tracks: migrated logging from Datadog to Coralogix via OpenTelemetry with a two-week retention policy (saving ~$60K/month), applied a systematic infrastructure audit to right-size AWS resources (~$60K/month), and cleaned up the communications engine's most expensive duplicate sends. Total confirmed savings: $120K+/month. I then led the AWS to GCP migration across Byju's group, completing 70% of critical services, and represented both Whitehat Jr. and Toppr in group-level CTO infrastructure reviews. The product turned profitable. The parent company collapsed at the holding company level — unrelated to product performance — and after months of salary delays I moved on.

Toppr — Tech Lead → Principal Engineer · 2019–2022

Toppr was an edtech platform for exam preparation — large user base, competitive SEO traffic, enormous question-and-answer content library. I joined as Tech Lead to build Toppr Answr, an SEO-driven Q&A platform. At the time it existed as a script. Within a year it was serving 4 million sessions per day and ranked as India's number-one education traffic platform and second globally in its category.

The first scaling crisis came when traffic grew faster than the architecture could handle and the team couldn't identify why. I diagnosed it as a Python GIL and WSGI threading bottleneck — the kind of problem that looks like many different symptoms depending on where you look. The fix wasn't a patch: I made the case for migrating Answr's backend to Go, led the migration, and watched compute drop from roughly 300 cores to 30 — about a 90% reduction — with p95 latency settling near 10ms. I co-wrote a detailed post-mortem and migration writeup that went into Toppr's engineering blog; it became the reference for how we handled language migrations across the org.

Later I led a platform-wide AWS cost audit — not as a cost-cutting exercise, but as a systematic ROI review: identify the biggest line items, evaluate utilisation, right-size or eliminate. The audit saved $80K/month. I published the framework internally so other teams could run the same exercise. I was promoted to Principal Engineer. Toppr's traffic growth — Answr was a major driver — made the company an attractive acquisition. Byju's acquired Toppr in 2021.

Transin Logistics — Analyst → Backend Engineer · 2017–2019

Transin was a logistics company moving freight across India. I joined as an analyst, hit a wall on the original mandate, and taught myself Node.js and backend development to build past it. The first problem I solved was accounting: every transaction generated manual reconciliation work downstream. I built an event-driven pipeline — DynamoDB Streams into Kafka into a finance service that wrote directly to QuickBooks — that eliminated 90% of the manual accounting load.

The second problem required leaving the office. Field agents weren't entering data into the internal tool. The instinct in the team was that it was a compliance problem. I went on-ground to a branch to watch how agents actually worked. The tool gave them nothing back — no confirmation, no visibility, no reason to trust it. I built role-based dashboards that gave agents real-time feedback on their own data. Adoption followed. That pattern — diagnose before prescribing, get close to the actual user — has shaped how I approach product engineering since. I also built TruckForce, a mobile-first field operations app. Company revenue grew from ₹70Cr to ₹150Cr during this period.

Eclerx — Software Engineer · 2015–2017

Eclerx is a financial services BPO. My first job. I joined as a fresh hire and built a centralised security monitoring pipeline across 9,000 machines — ELK stack, Hadoop, Tableau — from scratch. The key decision was introducing Kafka as a durability buffer between the log collectors and Elasticsearch. We were losing logs whenever Elasticsearch nodes went down, and the retry logic at the edges wasn't reliable. Nobody asked for Kafka specifically; it was the right answer to the problem. It held. I won a Star Performer award and presented the architecture at an industry conference. I left because the feedback loops in a 9,000-person org were too slow — I wanted to build things where users could tell you the same day whether it worked.
