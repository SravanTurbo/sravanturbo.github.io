Why we stopped using consumer lag as a health signal

2 June 2026 incident · kafka · observability

For two years our on-call runbook said the same thing: if consumer lag on the ingestion topic crosses 50k messages, page the on-call engineer. It was one of the first alerts we wrote when we moved to Kafka. It fired constantly — sometimes correctly, usually not. We kept tuning the threshold. Eventually we deleted the alert entirely and replaced it with something that actually worked.

This is the story of why, and what we learned about the difference between a metric that looks like a health signal and one that is a health signal.

What we were trying to measure

The service in question consumed events from an upstream producer, applied a lightweight enrichment, and wrote results to a downstream database. End-to-end latency mattered: if events sat in the topic for more than a few minutes, a downstream reporting job would miss its SLA window.

Consumer lag — the difference between the latest offset produced and the last offset committed by our consumer — seemed like the natural proxy for that latency. More lag means the consumer is further behind, which means higher end-to-end latency. Simple.

The problem is that lag is an inventory measure, not a flow measure. It tells you how many messages are waiting. It doesn't tell you whether the consumer is keeping up, falling behind, or idle because the producer went quiet.

The incident that clarified things

On a Tuesday evening the alert fired. Lag had climbed from a few hundred to 800k messages in about twenty minutes. The on-call engineer — me — woke up, checked the consumer, found it healthy: CPU normal, no errors, processing at its usual rate of roughly 12k messages per second.

The producer had spiked. A batch job upstream had dumped a backlog of six hours of accumulated events into the topic at once. The consumer was fine. It would catch up in about a minute. The alert was noise.

That incident was easy to diagnose. What made us finally delete the alert was a different pattern we noticed reviewing the prior six months of pages: nearly half our lag alerts had fired during periods when the producer had spiked, not when the consumer had slowed. The consumer was healthy every time. We were paging engineers to investigate a queue filling up faster than usual, which is not a problem.

The fix: measure what you actually care about

We wanted to know whether the consumer was keeping up with the producer — not whether lag was high in absolute terms. Those are different questions.

We replaced the lag threshold alert with two signals:

Consumer throughput drop. Alert if the consumer's message-per-second rate falls more than 40% below its trailing 10-minute average, sustained for 3 minutes. This fires when the consumer slows down. It does not fire when the producer speeds up.

End-to-end event age. We started stamping events with a producer-side timestamp and measuring the age of the oldest uncommitted event in the topic. Alert if any event is older than 8 minutes. This directly measures the latency we actually cared about.

The second signal required a small schema change and some coordination with the upstream team. It took an extra week. It was worth it. The age alert fires precisely when the downstream SLA is at risk — not before, not on noise.

The broader lesson

Lag is a useful diagnostic tool. When an alert fires and you're trying to understand what happened, knowing the lag curve helps you reconstruct the timeline. But as an alerting signal it has the wrong shape: it rises during both producer spikes (fine) and consumer slowdowns (not fine), and it gives you no information about actual end-to-end latency because it doesn't know anything about message age.

The general pattern: if your alert is a count or a queue depth, ask whether you're measuring inventory or flow. Inventory measures go up for many reasons, only some of which require a human. Flow measures — throughput, age, error rate — tend to change only when something is actually wrong.

We still expose consumer lag on our dashboards. It's one of the first things I look at during an incident. It just doesn't wake anyone up anymore.
